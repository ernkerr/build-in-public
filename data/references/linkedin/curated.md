## Closest voice matches (use these as north star)

### Ref A — "AI-first dev flow: LOC generated vs. LOC reviewed"

```
One of the things that I think needs to be talked about more in an ai-first development flow (especially for solo projects) is the balance between LOC generated vs. LOC reviewed.

Since I've started coding predominantly with AI, the experience I have is much closer to that of a senior engineer breaking down tickets and reviewing prs. Which is to say: it feels (amazingly) like I have a team, or at least one dedicated engineer working with me. This part is awesome, because managed correctly, I can multiply my output across as many good tickets as I can write.

But what doesn't get talked about enough is how much of a partner I need to be to that engineer. I need to review the code, sanity check the changes, and catch things that they didn't see. Often times we talk about this in terms of the LLM 'hallucinating' or 'crashing out', but in practice both humans and LLMs write a lot of functional code that could be better or creates subtle (or not so subtle) bugs. Great engineers break production every day, and it takes another great engineer to reflect on their code to make it better.

My functional velocity is easily 8x using AI if I counted committed and tested LOC. But I think engineers could do a lot to talk about themselves as reviewers and planners rather than typists, because LOC generated is a very false and misleading metric that betrays what we're doing out here and the value that engineers add when partnered with an LLM. My 2C!
```

Why it works:
- Opens with a genuine observation ("One of the things I think needs to be talked about more...")
- Thinks out loud, shows its work — not a polished thesis
- Uses parentheticals to add warmth ("(amazingly)", "(or not so subtle)")
- Signs off casually ("My 2C!")
- Specific number (8x) lands because it's used in service of a broader point
- No guru framing, no rhetorical CTA

### Ref B — "Cognitive offloading / spreadsheets for travel"

```
Long before Google Maps let us organize lists by activity, I used to make a spreadsheet for every city I visited.

I like to make loose plans (outlined with details like ticketing information on another tab) and then pull up my main spreadsheet to see what's nearby wherever I happen to wander.

Is it excessive? Probably.
Is it wildly helpful? Absolutely.

There's actually research on this — it's called cognitive offloading. Your brain is terrible at remembering details, so you externalize them.

The Michael Gerlich paper on AI and cognitive offloading talks about how delegating thinking to AI erodes critical thinking. But here's the thing: my spreadsheet doesn't think for me. It just remembers the ramen place I bookmarked six months ago so I don't have to.

I still choose where to go. I still wander. I still discover.

The spreadsheet just handles the "wait, what was that place called again?" so I can stay in flow instead of frantically Googling when I'm hungry and decision-fatigued.

It's the difference between offloading memory (smart) and offloading judgment (dangerous). One frees up mental space. The other atrophies your brain.
```

Why it works:
- Personal anecdote sets up a technical/philosophical point
- "Is it excessive? Probably. / Is it wildly helpful? Absolutely." — self-aware, casual rhythm
- Grounds the insight in research without sounding academic
- Closes with a clean distinction, not a CTA or lesson
- The insight *emerges* from the story rather than being announced upfront

## Hook patterns across references

- Decisive number + action: "I deleted 13,000 lines of my own code this week."
- Problem framing: "There's a gap in how most teams handle X that nobody's really building for."
- Casual timing: "~20 hours into a side project..."
- Contrarian jab: "A developer with half your experience is making more money than you right now."
- Scene-setting: "When I moved back to SF... I expected SF to be 'back'..."
- Humor setup: "I asked ChatGPT to roast me. It didn't hesitate."

## Structural moves to borrow

- Rhetorical question pairs: "Is it excessive? Probably. / Is it wildly helpful? Absolutely."
- Thinking-out-loud parentheticals: "(amazingly)", "(or not so subtle)"
- Casual sign-offs: "My 2C!", "Oh well... Happy Monday"
- Specific bullet lists of real features/examples
- Ending with a distinction rather than a CTA
- Genuine question at the end (not rhetorical) — invites real replies

## Patterns to AVOID

- "Not because X. Not because Y. Because Z." — overused guru cadence
- "Here's the truth most developers don't want to hear:" — guru framing
- "The hardest part wasn't X. It was Y." — Instagram-quote territory
- Rhetorical CTAs like "What's the simplest form your product could take?"
- Em dashes mid-sentence (Erin prefers commas, parens, or "w/")
